Information Design and Mechanism Design: An Integrated Framework
arxiv • Dirk Bergemann, Tibor Heumann, Stephen Morris • 2026-01-24
Score: 18 mechanism design, auction theory, screening
This paper examines how a designer can jointly optimize mechanism design (allocation rules) and information design (information revelation) in screening environments. The methodology is theoretical, using majorization theory and quantile functions to show that both problems reduce to maximizing linear functionals subject to majorization constraints—mechanisms choose allocations majorized by inventory, while information design chooses structures majorized by the prior. The key finding is that when jointly optimizing both mechanisms and information structures, pooling of values and allocations is always optimal, providing a unified framework that extends classic auction theory results and reveals new insights about welfare effects of simultaneous allocation and information design.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite catastrophic AGI risks, contrary to standard real options theory which predicts delay under high uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors model the AGI race where failure costs include a global systemic ruin parameter (D) rather than just sunk investment costs. The key finding is that shared catastrophic risk creates a "suicide region" where competitive pressures force rational actors to deploy AGI systems early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV, because the risk term cancels out of equilibrium conditions when both players face the same existential threat.
Dynamic Mechanism Design without Monetary Transfers: A Queueing Theory Approach
arxiv • Zihao Li, Xuandong Chen • 2026-01-28
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines the optimal design of allocation mechanisms when agents and goods arrive stochastically over time, agents have private information about their types, and monetary transfers are unavailable. Using queueing theory, the authors characterize the steady-state optimal mechanism as a dynamic threshold policy where admission and allocation decisions depend on state-dependent type thresholds and costly verification options. The key contribution is proving that simple threshold rules are optimal in dynamic settings without transfers, with applications to public housing, grants, and internal capital allocation where prices cannot be used to balance supply and demand.
Managing Learning Structures
arxiv • Hiroto Sato, Ryo Shirakawa • 2025-12-23
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines how a designer should optimally manage information transmission when agents have partial private information about a common-value good and the designer wants to maximize allocation without monetary transfers. Using mechanism design theory, the authors characterize optimal mechanisms under general information distributions, showing they can be summarized by two key parameters governing allocation probability and learning intensity. The key finding is that optimal mechanisms sometimes withhold socially efficient allocations to maintain proper learning incentives, demonstrating a fundamental trade-off between immediate efficiency and information management in mechanism design.
Sharing with Frictions: Limited Transfers and Costly Inspections
arxiv • Federico Bobbio, Randall A. Berry, Michael L. Honig, Thanh Nguyen et al. • 2025-12-25
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper addresses the optimal allocation of government-held spectrum between non-commercial incumbents (federal agencies, defense) and commercial users when direct monetary transfers are prohibited and both parties have private information about their valuations. The authors use theoretical mechanism design to characterize the optimal allocation mechanism under these constraints with asymmetric information. The key contribution is developing a novel mechanism that achieves efficient resource sharing without monetary transfers while addressing the dual information asymmetry problem inherent in spectrum sharing arrangements.
