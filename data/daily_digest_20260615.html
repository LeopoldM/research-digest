Battery Bidding under Price Uncertainty in Wholesale Electricity Markets
arxiv • Vincent Yinjun-Wang, Madeleine Udell • 2026-06-12
Score: 11 wholesale electricity, market power
This paper investigates why grid-scale batteries exhibit apparent withholding behavior in wholesale electricity markets, asking whether such patterns reflect strategic manipulation or rational operations under uncertainty. The authors develop a theoretical asset-level model where price-taking batteries optimize stepwise bid curves under price uncertainty using a mean-CVaR objective, then solve this as a linear program and apply it empirically. The key finding is that apparent withholding can arise purely from rational risk management without market power, as batteries balance scarce energy storage against uncertain future prices, with uncertainty effects that depend critically on the state of charge and create layered bidding structures.
From Trade War to Green Transition: Optimal Electric Vehicle Tariffs with Revenue-Funded Subsidies -- by Panle Jia Barwick, Jack Collison, Pinelopi K. Goldberg, Shanjun Li, Yucheng Wang
nber
Score: 11 green transition, oligopoly
This paper examines the optimal design of trade policy for electric vehicles when governments balance environmental objectives with traditional welfare goals. The authors develop a theoretical framework combining differentiated-product oligopoly with a structural demand model estimated using vehicle-level data from 13 countries to determine welfare-maximizing tariffs and subsidies. The key finding is that the optimal policy combines moderate tariffs on imported EVs with revenue-funded domestic production subsidies, which significantly outperforms both protectionist and laissez-faire approaches by more than doubling EV market share while generating substantial welfare gains and emissions reductions.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses how to select optimal future uses for underperforming real estate assets requiring redevelopment by developing a decision framework that accounts for irreversibility and uncertainty. The methodology combines real options theory with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to evaluate alternative uses across dimensions of economic value, risk, complexity, and time-to-income while preserving managerial flexibility. The key contribution is operationalizing option value preservation in redevelopment decisions through systematic ex-ante screening that reduces project over-complexification and use-demand misalignment.
Strategic Inertia and Institutional Change:A Behavioral Model of Price Reforms versus Action Deletion
arxiv • Madjid Eshaghi Gordji, Mohammadali Berahman, Hasti Eshaghi • 2026-05-20
Score: 7 climate policy
This paper examines why inefficient institutions persist and compares the effectiveness of different policy interventions to overcome institutional inertia. The authors develop a quantal response equilibrium model with status-quo bias, incorporating switching costs for deviating from inherited default actions, and analyze this framework using a binary coordination game with theoretical proofs and numerical simulations. The key finding is that while price-based reforms (taxes) only succeed above a critical threshold regardless of bounded rationality levels, completely banning the inefficient option (action deletion) always guarantees transition to the superior equilibrium and yields higher expected welfare than any finite tax when the superior equilibrium is Pareto-dominant.
Breaking Status-Quo Inertia in Living Temporal Games: Dynamic Intervention, Implementation, and Structural Design
arxiv • Madjid Eshaghi Gordji, Ali Jabbari, Mohammad Ali Berahman, Esmaiel Abounoori • 2026-05-18
Score: 7 mechanism design
This paper examines how a social planner can design dynamic interventions to eliminate inefficient status-quo equilibria in continuous-time stochastic games where agents strategically control their activity states on temporal networks. Using theoretical analysis, the authors prove a threshold theorem showing that bounded price transfers fail below a critical magnitude, establish structural dominance results demonstrating that network modifications (replacing continuous-flow with discrete-transport edges) can succeed where price interventions with any finite budget fail, and derive impossibility results for mechanism design under private information. The key contribution is showing that structural network changes can overcome status-quo inertia more effectively than traditional price-based interventions, with discrete-transport modifications expanding the set of implementable efficient outcomes.
