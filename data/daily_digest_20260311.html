The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper investigates why the US and China accelerate AI investment despite catastrophic AGI risks, contradicting standard real options theory that predicts delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs (ruin parameter D) are globally shared, risk terms cancel out of equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where rational actors deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that competitive dynamics can force premature deployment of potentially catastrophic technologies, and only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore efficient investment timing.
Existence of Equilibrium Mechanisms in Generalized Principal-Agent Problems with Interacting Teams
arxiv • Brian Roberson • 2026-02-23
Score: 9 mechanism design, principal-agent
This paper addresses the fundamental question of when equilibrium exists in multi-principal mechanism design games where teams interact strategically and each principal's feasible mechanisms depend on other principals' choices. The authors develop a novel theoretical approach that simultaneously tracks truthful outcome distributions and deviation sets to overcome discontinuities in incentive-compatible mechanism correspondences that can cause equilibrium non-existence (as in Myerson 1982). The key contribution is establishing general sufficient conditions for equilibrium existence in these complex strategic environments, providing theoretical foundations for analyzing mechanism design with interacting teams and spillover effects.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses how to optimally select the intended use for underperforming real estate assets requiring redevelopment, treating this as a strategic option selection problem rather than a design optimization issue. The methodology combines real options theory (accounting for irreversibility and managerial flexibility) with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to evaluate alternatives across dimensions of economic value, risk, complexity, and time-to-income. The key contribution is a decision-analytic framework that preserves option value through disciplined ex-ante screening, demonstrated to reduce over-complexification and use-demand misalignment across diverse asset types and urban contexts.
Competition in Health Insurance Markets -- by Martin Gaynor, Amanda Starc
nber
Score: 9 adverse selection, market power
This paper investigates how market concentration and asymmetric information interact to affect competition and welfare in U.S. health insurance markets. Using empirical analysis of commercial, Medicare Advantage, and Medicaid markets, the authors document increasing concentration and demonstrate that insurer consolidation leads to higher premiums through the interaction of adverse selection and market power. The key contribution is showing how information asymmetries amplify the welfare losses from market concentration, with implications for antitrust policy, risk adjustment mechanisms, and regulatory design in health insurance markets.
Delegation in Strategic Environments and Equilibrium Uniqueness
arxiv • Fedor Sandomirskiy, Ben Wincelberg • 2026-02-25
Score: 7 mechanism design
This paper investigates when strategic games have unique equilibrium predictions under delegation to intermediaries, addressing equilibrium multiplicity concerns in mechanism design. The methodology is theoretical, characterizing games where coarse correlated equilibria (CCE) or individually rational correlated profiles (IRCP) are unique depending on whether intermediaries can punish deviations. The key contribution is establishing structural connections between these delegation-based solution concepts and providing new uniqueness conditions that don't require dominant strategies, yielding equilibria robust to information, perturbations, and learning dynamics with applications to collusion-proof mechanisms.
