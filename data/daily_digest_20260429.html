Mapping the causal structure of price formation in Texas's transitioning electricity market
arxiv • Shiva Madadkhani, Nils Sturma, Mathias Drton, Svetlana Ikonnikova • 2026-04-15
Score: 20 electricity market, wholesale electricity, renewable integration
This study investigates how the causal structure of electricity price formation is evolving in Texas's renewable-transitioning market by examining relationships among wind generation, natural gas prices, and demand. Using causal discovery methods applied to wholesale electricity market data, the authors identify time-varying causal relationships between key price drivers. The key finding is that wind generation has become the dominant causal driver of day-ahead prices (with effects 3x larger than gas prices), fundamentally overturning the traditional view of Texas as a gas-price-driven market and revealing complex spatial redistribution effects of renewable generation on congestion costs.
Truthful Production Uncertainty in Electricity Markets: A Two-Stage Mechanism
arxiv • Shobhit Singhal, Lesia Mitridati, Licio Romao • 2026-04-02
Score: 13 market clearing, electricity market
This paper addresses how to efficiently incorporate production uncertainty from renewable generators into electricity market clearing when current day-ahead markets only elicit point forecasts, ignoring uncertainty that drives costly real-time balancing needs. The authors develop a theoretical two-stage mechanism where producers report their full production forecast distributions day-ahead followed by realized output, extending Vickrey-Clarke-Groves payments to ensure truthful reporting of uncertainty and individual rationality. The key contribution is proving this mechanism achieves incentive compatibility for uncertainty revelation and demonstrating through case studies that accounting for forecast distributions in day-ahead clearing reduces total system costs compared to current point-forecast approaches.
Strategic Bidding in 6G Spectrum Auctions with Large Language Models
arxiv • Ismail Lotfi, Ali Ghrayeb • 2026-04-27
Score: 13 mechanism design, bidding behavior
This paper investigates how Large Language Models can serve as strategic bidding agents in repeated 6G spectrum auctions for vehicular networks, comparing their performance against the truthful Vickrey-Clarke-Groves mechanism and heuristic strategies. The methodology combines mechanism design theory with empirical evaluation of LLM agents that use historical data and prompt-based reasoning to optimize long-term utility under budget constraints. The key finding is that while LLMs approximate VCG equilibria when theoretical assumptions hold, they outperform traditional approaches when assumptions break down (e.g., under budget constraints), demonstrating superior adaptability and sustained participation in dynamic auction environments.
Lexicographic Robustness and the Efficiency of Optimal Mechanisms
arxiv • Ashwin Kambhampati • 2026-04-07
Score: 11 mechanism design, screening
This paper addresses how to select robust mechanisms when facing uncertainty about the economic environment, proposing a lexicographic refinement of the standard maxmin criterion to narrow down the typically large set of maxmin-optimal mechanisms. The methodology is theoretical, developing the concept of "proper robustness" as the strongest lexicographic refinement and characterizing optimal mechanisms in screening, auction, and public goods settings. The key finding is that proper robustness yields ex post efficient mechanisms in screening and auction environments, but identifies specific optimal inefficiencies in public goods provision that worsen significantly as economy size increases.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite acknowledging catastrophic AGI risks, contrary to standard real options theory that predicts delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors model the AGI race where failure costs include a global systemic ruin parameter (D) correlated with development velocity. The key finding is that shared catastrophic risk creates a "suicide region" where competitive pressures force rational agents to deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV, as the common risk term cancels out of equilibrium conditions, and this race can only be halted by internalizing ruin costs through private liability mechanisms.
