Information Design and Mechanism Design: An Integrated Framework
arxiv • Dirk Bergemann, Tibor Heumann, Stephen Morris • 2026-01-24
Score: 18 mechanism design, auction theory, screening
This paper examines how a designer can jointly optimize allocation mechanisms and information structures in screening environments with quasilinear utility. Using majorization theory and quantile functions, the authors show that both mechanism design (choosing allocations majorized by inventory) and information design (choosing information structures majorized by the prior) can be formulated as linear functionals subject to majorization constraints, making the joint problem bilinear. The key finding is that pooling of agent values and corresponding allocations is always optimal when simultaneously designing both mechanisms and information structures, unifying and extending classical auction theory results while providing new welfare insights.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper investigates why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite catastrophic risks, contrary to real options theory which predicts delay under high uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors model the AGI race where failure costs include a global systemic ruin parameter (D) rather than just sunk investment costs (I). The key finding is that shared catastrophic risk creates a "suicide region" where competitive pressures force rational agents to deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV, as the risk term cancels out of equilibrium conditions—only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore safety incentives.
Dynamic Mechanism Design without Monetary Transfers: A Queueing Theory Approach
arxiv • Zihao Li, Xuandong Chen • 2026-01-28
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines how to optimally allocate stochastically arriving agents and goods when monetary transfers are unavailable but costly type verification is possible. Using dynamic mechanism design with queueing theory, the authors characterize the steady-state optimal mechanism as a state-dependent threshold policy where admission and allocation decisions depend on current queue lengths and private type thresholds. The key contribution is showing that without monetary transfers, optimal mechanisms take the form of dynamic thresholds that balance verification costs against improved matching quality, with applications to public housing, grants, and internal capital allocation.
The Algorithmic Barrier: Quantifying Artificial Frictional Unemployment in Automated Recruitment Systems
arxiv • Ibrahim Denis Fofanah • 2026-01-20
Score: 9 screening, information asymmetry
This paper investigates whether automated recruitment systems using keyword-based screening contribute to artificially high frictional unemployment by creating semantic mismatches between job requirements and candidate qualifications. The authors employ controlled simulations comparing traditional keyword screening with semantic vector-based matching of resumes and job descriptions. The key finding is that semantic matching substantially improves recall and matching efficiency without sacrificing precision, suggesting that outdated algorithmic hiring practices impose significant economic costs through suboptimal labor market matching.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses how to systematically select optimal redevelopment uses for underperforming real estate assets experiencing structural demand misalignment rather than cyclical downturns. The methodology develops a theoretical decision-analytic framework that integrates real options theory (emphasizing irreversibility and managerial flexibility) with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to evaluate alternatives across dimensions of economic value, risk, complexity, and time-to-income. The key contribution is operationalizing option value preservation through disciplined ex-ante screening that treats redevelopment as strategic option selection rather than design optimization, demonstrating reduced over-complexification and improved alignment across different asset types and urban contexts.
