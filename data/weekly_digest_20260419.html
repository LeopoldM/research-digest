Mapping the causal structure of price formation in Texas's transitioning electricity market
arxiv • Shiva Madadkhani, Nils Sturma, Mathias Drton, Svetlana Ikonnikova • 2026-04-15
Score: 20 electricity market, wholesale electricity, renewable integration
This paper investigates how the causal structure of electricity price formation is evolving in Texas's rapidly changing wholesale market driven by renewable integration and demand growth. Using causal discovery methods applied to supply- and demand-side price determinants, the authors find that wind generation has become the dominant causal driver of day-ahead prices—with effects over 3 times larger than natural gas prices—fundamentally overturning the traditional view of Texas as a gas-price-driven market. The key contribution demonstrates that wind reduces local prices but redistributes congestion costs across regions in seasonally varying patterns, highlighting the need for dynamic causal models to understand price formation during the energy transition.
Truthful Production Uncertainty in Electricity Markets: A Two-Stage Mechanism
arxiv • Shobhit Singhal, Lesia Mitridati, Licio Romao • 2026-04-02
Score: 13 market clearing, electricity market
This paper addresses how to incorporate production uncertainty from renewable sources into electricity market clearing mechanisms by having producers report their entire forecast distribution rather than just point estimates. The methodology develops a theoretical two-stage mechanism extending Vickery-Clarke-Groves (VCG) payments, where producers submit forecast distributions day-ahead and realized production real-time, with formal proofs of incentive compatibility and individual rationality. The key contribution is demonstrating that this mechanism enables market operators to optimize dispatch and ancillary service procurement while accounting for balancing costs, resulting in reduced total system costs compared to current point-forecast markets, particularly in renewable-heavy systems.
Carbon Regulation and Competition in the European Airline Industry
arxiv • Ertian Chen, Lichao Chen, Lars Nesheim • 2026-03-29
Score: 13 carbon pricing, emissions trading
This paper examines how EU carbon pricing through the Emissions Trading System affects competition and welfare in European airline markets. Using a structural two-stage model of airline competition estimated on European market share and pricing data, the authors simulate counterfactual scenarios with endogenous route entry, frequency, and pricing decisions. The key finding is that carbon regulation creates highly asymmetric impacts: consumer surplus falls up to 25% overall (90% in medium-haul markets), airline profits decline 8-45%, but short-haul markets experience net welfare gains as airlines reallocate capacity, with carbon revenues ($0.9-3.1B) and avoided emissions benefits ($0.5-1.4B) partially offsetting losses.
Lexicographic Robustness and the Efficiency of Optimal Mechanisms
arxiv • Ashwin Kambhampati • 2026-04-07
Score: 11 mechanism design, screening
This paper addresses how to design mechanisms that perform robustly when the designer is uncertain about the economic environment. The authors develop a lexicographic refinement of the standard maxmin robustness criterion and theoretically characterize the efficiency properties of the resulting optimal mechanisms. The key finding is that the strongest refinement (proper robustness) yields ex post efficient mechanisms in standard screening and auction settings, but identifies specific optimal inefficiencies that become severe with economy size in public goods provision.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite acknowledging catastrophic AGI risks, contrary to standard real options theory which predicts delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs are shared globally, the risk term cancels out of equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where competitive pressures force rational actors to deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore option value and prevent premature deployment, as warning shots fail to alter the winner-takes-all race dynamics.
