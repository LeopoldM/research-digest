Information Design and Mechanism Design: An Integrated Framework
arxiv • Dirk Bergemann, Tibor Heumann, Stephen Morris • 2026-01-24
Score: 18 mechanism design, auction theory, screening
This paper examines how to jointly optimize mechanism design (allocation rules) and information design (information structures) in screening environments with quasilinear utility. Using majorization theory and quantile functions, the authors show that both problems reduce to maximizing linear functionals subject to majorization constraints—mechanisms choose allocations majorized by inventory, while information design chooses structures majorized by priors. The key finding is that when jointly optimized, the bilinear problem with dual majorization constraints always yields pooling of values and allocations as optimal, unifying auction theory with information design and revealing new welfare implications.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite catastrophic AGI risks, contrary to standard real options theory that predicts delay under high uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs are shared globally, risk terms cancel out of equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where rational actors deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that competitive pressures can force premature deployment of potentially catastrophic technologies, and that only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore rational delay incentives.
Dynamic Mechanism Design without Monetary Transfers: A Queueing Theory Approach
arxiv • Zihao Li, Xuandong Chen • 2026-01-28
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines how to optimally allocate resources when agents and goods arrive stochastically over time, agents have private information about their types, and monetary transfers are prohibited. Using a queueing-theoretic framework with costly verification and queue holding costs, the authors characterize the optimal steady-state mechanism as a dynamic threshold policy where admission and allocation thresholds depend on current queue states. The key contribution is demonstrating that state-dependent thresholds outperform static rules in non-monetary allocation problems, with applications to public housing, grants, and internal capital budgeting.
Managing Learning Structures
arxiv • Hiroto Sato, Ryo Shirakawa • 2025-12-23
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines how a designer should optimally allocate a common-value good to agents with private information when monetary transfers are unavailable and the goal is maximizing allocations. Using mechanism design theory, the authors characterize an optimal allocation mechanism with two key parameters controlling allocation probability and induced learning intensity. The main finding is that optimal learning management requires the designer to strategically withhold allocations even when they would be socially efficient, demonstrating a fundamental tension between immediate allocation goals and maintaining proper incentives for information revelation and learning.
Sharing with Frictions: Limited Transfers and Costly Inspections
arxiv • Federico Bobbio, Randall A. Berry, Michael L. Honig, Thanh Nguyen et al. • 2025-12-25
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper addresses how to efficiently allocate government-held spectrum between non-commercial incumbents (federal agencies, defense, etc.) and commercial users when direct monetary transfers are prohibited and both parties have private information about their valuations. The authors use mechanism design theory to characterize the optimal allocation mechanism under these constraints. The key contribution is developing a novel mechanism that achieves efficient resource sharing without monetary transfers by using costly inspections as an alternative instrument, with applications extending beyond spectrum to other government-held resources.
