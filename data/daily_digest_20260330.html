Salience and (Non-)Buyer's Remorse: Optimal Nonlinear Pricing with Cognitively Constrained Consumers -- by Aaron L. Bodoh-Creed, Brent R. Hickman, John A. List, Ian Muir, Gregory K. Sun
nber
Score: 9 self-selection, screening
This paper examines how cognitive constraints affect consumer self-selection in nonlinear pricing schemes, using large-scale field experiments with Lyft's subscription service. The authors employ a structural model combined with a novel identification strategy that treats subscription uptake as noncompliance in a binary-instrument framework, leveraging LATE methods to recover utility parameters and behavioral biases. They find that salience failures and forecast errors significantly explain systematic deviations from optimal self-selection (high-demand consumers rejecting profitable subscriptions and low-demand consumers choosing unprofitable ones), and show that while nonlinear pricing can generate substantial welfare gains over linear pricing, these gains are highly sensitive to behavioral deviations from rational choice.
