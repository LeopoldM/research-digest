Information Design and Mechanism Design: An Integrated Framework
arxiv • Dirk Bergemann, Tibor Heumann, Stephen Morris • 2026-01-24
Score: 18 mechanism design, auction theory, screening
This paper investigates how to jointly optimize mechanism design (allocation rules) and information design (information revelation) in screening environments with quasilinear utility. Using majorization theory and quantile functions, the authors show that both problems reduce to maximizing linear functionals subject to majorization constraints, with mechanism design constrained by inventory majorization and information design constrained by prior distribution majorization. The key finding is that when both mechanism and information structure are optimized simultaneously, pooling of values and allocations is always optimal, unifying and extending classical auction theory results while providing new insights into welfare effects of joint optimization.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite catastrophic AGI risks, contradicting standard real options theory that predicts investment delay under high uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs are shared globally, the risk term cancels out of players' equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where rational actors deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that competitive pressures override safety considerations in winner-takes-all races with systemic risks, and that only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore rational investment timing.
Existence of Equilibrium Mechanisms in Generalized Principal-Agent Problems with Interacting Teams
arxiv • Brian Roberson • 2026-02-23
Score: 9 mechanism design, principal-agent
This paper addresses the equilibrium existence problem in multi-principal mechanism design where teams have strategic spillovers across principals. The authors develop a novel theoretical approach that tracks both truthful equilibrium outcomes and feasible deviation sets to characterize the correspondence of incentive-compatible mechanisms, resolving discontinuities that typically prevent equilibrium existence in these games. The key contribution is establishing general sufficient conditions for equilibrium existence in generalized principal-agent problems with interacting teams, providing theoretical foundations for analyzing complex multi-principal environments with team production and strategic interdependencies.
Dynamic Mechanism Design without Monetary Transfers: A Queueing Theory Approach
arxiv • Zihao Li, Xuandong Chen • 2026-01-28
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines optimal allocation mechanisms when agents and goods arrive stochastically over time, agents have private types affecting principal payoff, and monetary transfers are unavailable but costly type verification is possible. Using dynamic mechanism design theory combined with queueing models, the authors characterize the optimal steady-state mechanism as a state-dependent threshold policy that determines agent admission and goods allocation based on current queue states. The key contribution is showing that optimal mechanisms in non-monetary, dynamic environments take the form of simple threshold rules that adapt to system congestion, with applications to public housing, grants, and internal capital allocation.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses how to select optimal land uses for underperforming real estate assets facing structural demand misalignment rather than cyclical market weakness. The methodology integrates real options theory with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to create a framework that evaluates redevelopment alternatives across four dimensions: expected economic value, market/operational risk, technical/managerial complexity, and time-to-income. The key contribution is operationalizing option value preservation through systematic ex-ante screening that treats redevelopment as strategic option selection rather than design optimization, demonstrated to reduce over-complexification and use-demand misalignment across different asset types and urban contexts.
