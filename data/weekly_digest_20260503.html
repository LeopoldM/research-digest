Mapping the causal structure of price formation in Texas's transitioning electricity market
arxiv • Shiva Madadkhani, Nils Sturma, Mathias Drton, Svetlana Ikonnikova • 2026-04-15
Score: 20 electricity market, wholesale electricity, renewable integration
This paper investigates how the causal relationships driving electricity price formation are evolving in Texas's rapidly changing wholesale electricity market, particularly with increased renewable penetration. Using causal discovery methods applied to supply- and demand-side variables, the authors find that wind generation has become the dominant causal driver of day-ahead prices—with effects over 3 times larger than natural gas prices—fundamentally overturning the traditional view of Texas as a gas-price-driven market. The key contribution demonstrates that wind reduces local prices but redistributes congestion costs across regions with seasonal variation, highlighting the need for time-varying causal models to understand price dynamics in transitioning electricity systems.
Truthful Production Uncertainty in Electricity Markets: A Two-Stage Mechanism
arxiv • Shobhit Singhal, Lesia Mitridati, Licio Romao • 2026-04-02
Score: 13 market clearing, electricity market
This paper addresses how electricity markets can better incorporate production uncertainty from renewable sources by having generators report probability distributions rather than point forecasts in day-ahead markets. The authors develop a theoretical two-stage mechanism extending Vickrey-Clarke-Groves (VCG) payments, where producers submit forecast distributions day-ahead and realized production real-time, proving the mechanism achieves incentive compatibility and individual rationality. The key contribution is demonstrating that truthful revelation of uncertainty enables market operators to optimize dispatch and ancillary services more efficiently, reducing total system costs compared to current point-forecast mechanisms.
Strategic Bidding in 6G Spectrum Auctions with Large Language Models
arxiv • Ismail Lotfi, Ali Ghrayeb • 2026-04-27
Score: 13 mechanism design, bidding behavior
This paper examines how Large Language Models can serve as strategic bidding agents in repeated 6G spectrum auctions for vehicular networks, comparing their performance against the truthful Vickrey-Clarke-Groves mechanism and heuristic strategies. Using a game-theoretic framework with budget-constrained rational players, the authors conduct computational experiments to evaluate LLM bidding behavior across different market conditions. The key finding is that while LLMs approximate truthful equilibria when VCG assumptions hold, they outperform traditional mechanisms when assumptions break down (e.g., under budget constraints), demonstrating superior adaptability and utility achievement through dynamic learning from historical auction outcomes.
Lexicographic Robustness and the Efficiency of Optimal Mechanisms
arxiv • Ashwin Kambhampati • 2026-04-07
Score: 11 mechanism design, screening
This paper addresses how to select robust mechanisms when facing uncertainty about the economic environment, proposing a lexicographic refinement of the standard maxmin robustness criterion to narrow down the typically large set of maxmin-optimal mechanisms. The methodology is theoretical, developing the concept of "proper robustness" as the strongest lexicographic refinement and characterizing optimal mechanisms in screening, auction, and public goods settings. The key finding is that proper robustness yields ex post efficient mechanisms in screening and auction environments, but identifies specific optimal inefficiencies in public goods provision that become severe as economy size increases.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper investigates why major powers are accelerating AI investment despite acknowledging catastrophic risks from artificial general intelligence (AGI) misalignment, contrary to standard real options theory that predicts investment delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs are globally shared, the risk term cancels out of players' equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where competitive pressures force early deployment despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that only internalizing ruin costs through private liability mechanisms can restore option value and halt premature AGI deployment, as "warning shots" fail to deter acceleration in winner-takes-all competition.
