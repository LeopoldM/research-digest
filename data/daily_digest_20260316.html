The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why major powers accelerate AI development despite acknowledging catastrophic risks from AGI misalignment, contradicting standard real options theory that predicts investment delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when failure costs include a shared global ruin parameter (D) rather than just sunk investment costs (I), the risk terms cancel out of players' equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where rational actors deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that competitive dynamics can force premature deployment of potentially catastrophic technologies unless ruin costs are internalized through private liability mechanisms that make safety research economically prerequisite.
Existence of Equilibrium Mechanisms in Generalized Principal-Agent Problems with Interacting Teams
arxiv • Brian Roberson • 2026-02-23
Score: 9 mechanism design, principal-agent
This paper examines the existence of equilibrium in multi-principal mechanism design problems where each principal designs incentives for their own team while facing strategic spillovers from other teams' mechanisms. The authors develop a novel theoretical approach that tracks both truthful equilibrium outcomes and achievable deviation outcomes to address discontinuities in incentive-compatible mechanism correspondences that can prevent equilibrium existence (as in Myerson 1982). The key contribution is establishing general sufficient conditions for equilibrium existence in these complex multi-team environments, providing theoretical foundations for analyzing mechanism design with interacting principals and teams.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses how to select optimal intended uses for underperforming real estate assets requiring redevelopment by developing a decision framework that accounts for irreversibility and uncertainty. The methodology combines real options theory with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to evaluate alternative uses based on expected economic value, risk, complexity, and time-to-income while preserving managerial flexibility. The key contribution is operationalizing option value preservation in redevelopment decisions by treating use selection as strategic option choice rather than purely financial optimization, demonstrated to reduce misalignment and over-complexification across different asset types and urban contexts.
Stochastic Optimization and Coupling
arxiv • Frank Yang, Kai Hao Yang • 2026-03-12
Score: 7 mechanism design
This paper examines stochastic optimization problems where linear functionals are maximized over probability measures constrained by integral stochastic orders in arbitrary dimensions. Using theoretical analysis, the authors prove that four key properties are equivalent: closure of test function cones under pointwise minimum, affine value functions, convex solution correspondences with decomposable extreme points, and existence of order-preserving couplings for all measure pairs. The main contribution is a generalization of Blackwell's theorem that completely characterizes experiment comparisons through both instrumental values and information technologies, with applications to information design, mechanism design, and decision theory.
Self-Confirming Mechanisms
arxiv • Zhiming Feng, Qingmin Liu • 2026-03-13
Score: 7 mechanism design
This paper examines mechanism design when the designer lacks prior knowledge of agents' type distributions and must learn from the mechanism's own generated data. Using a theoretical approach, the authors develop the concept of self-confirming mechanisms where the designer's beliefs about agent types are consistent with the information revealed through mechanism outcomes, and prove a "fictitious revelation principle" showing any incentive-compatible mechanism can be represented as a direct mechanism with appropriately filtered type reports. The key contribution is demonstrating that in monopoly settings, dominant-strategy self-confirming mechanisms are equivalent to posted-price mechanisms with locally revenue-maximizing prices, providing a theoretical foundation for why simple pricing may emerge when designers learn from market behavior.
