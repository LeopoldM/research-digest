Carbon Regulation and Competition in the European Airline Industry
arxiv • Ertian Chen, Lichao Chen, Lars Nesheim • 2026-03-29
Score: 13 carbon pricing, emissions trading
This paper examines how EU carbon pricing under the Emissions Trading System affects competition and welfare in European aviation markets. The authors use a structural two-stage model of airline competition estimated on European market share and pricing data, incorporating endogenous decisions on route entry, flight frequency, and pricing. The key finding is that carbon regulation creates highly asymmetric impacts: consumer surplus falls up to 25% overall (90% in medium-haul markets), airline profits decline 8-45%, but short-haul markets experience net welfare gains as airlines reallocate capacity toward shorter routes, with carbon revenues of $0.9-3.1 billion partially offsetting welfare losses.
Prices vs. Quantities: Robust Regulation
arxiv • Zi Yang Kang • 2026-03-16
Score: 11 mechanism design, screening
This paper examines the optimal choice between price and quantity instruments for regulating consumption externalities when a regulator faces uncertainty about the correlation between individual demand and marginal externalities. Using a robust mechanism design approach where the regulator maximizes worst-case welfare across all possible correlations, the analysis shows that quantity controls (floors for positive externalities, ceilings for negative) are optimal under complete uncertainty. When the sign of the demand-externality correlation is known, uniform taxes or subsidies can be optimal, providing a welfare-theoretic justification for the widespread use of quantity-based regulations over price instruments.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite acknowledging catastrophic AGI risks, contrary to standard real options theory which predicts delay under high uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs are globally shared, the risk terms cancel out of players' equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where competitive pressures force early deployment despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore option value and prevent premature AGI deployment, as "warning shots" fail to deter acceleration in winner-takes-all competition.
