The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AGI development despite catastrophic risks, contradicting standard real options theory that predicts investment delay under high uncertainty. The authors develop a continuous-time preemption game model where both players face shared existential risk (parameter D) that scales with development speed, causing the risk terms to cancel out in equilibrium conditions. The key finding is that competitive dynamics create a "suicide region" where rational actors deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted returns, and this race can only be stopped by internalizing ruin costs through sufficient private liability mechanisms.
Existence of Equilibrium Mechanisms in Generalized Principal-Agent Problems with Interacting Teams
arxiv • Brian Roberson • 2026-02-23
Score: 9 mechanism design, principal-agent
This paper addresses the equilibrium existence problem in multi-principal mechanism design games where teams with internal agency problems interact through strategic spillovers. The authors develop a theoretical framework that tracks both truthful-obedient outcome distributions and deviations to resolve discontinuities in incentive-compatible mechanism correspondences that can prevent equilibrium existence (as in Myerson 1982). The key contribution is establishing general sufficient conditions for equilibrium existence in these complex environments by providing a novel approach to handle the interdependence between principals' mechanism choices when each faces internal team incentive compatibility constraints.
Dynamic Mechanism Design without Monetary Transfers: A Queueing Theory Approach
arxiv • Zihao Li, Xuandong Chen • 2026-01-28
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines optimal allocation mechanism design when agents and goods arrive stochastically over time, agents have private information about their types, and monetary transfers are unavailable but costly type verification is possible. Using queueing theory, the authors theoretically characterize the steady-state optimal mechanism as a dynamic threshold policy where admission and allocation thresholds depend on current queue states (numbers of agents and goods waiting). The key contribution is showing that state-dependent thresholds optimally balance the trade-offs between waiting costs, verification costs, and allocation efficiency in dynamic environments without monetary instruments, with applications to public housing, grants, and organizational capital budgeting.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses the problem of selecting optimal land uses for underperforming real estate redevelopment by developing a decision framework that accounts for irreversibility and uncertainty. The methodology combines real options theory with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to evaluate alternative uses across dimensions of economic value, risk, complexity, and timing while preserving managerial flexibility. The key contribution is demonstrating how integrating real options logic with structured multi-criteria evaluation can reduce strategic misalignment and over-complexification in redevelopment decisions through disciplined ex-ante screening.
Delegation in Strategic Environments and Equilibrium Uniqueness
arxiv • Fedor Sandomirskiy, Ben Wincelberg • 2026-02-25
Score: 7 mechanism design
This paper investigates when normal-form games have unique equilibrium predictions when players can delegate decisions to intermediaries with varying enforcement capabilities. The methodology is purely theoretical, modeling delegation outcomes through coarse correlated equilibria (CCE) for intermediaries without punishment power and individually rational correlated profiles (IRCP) for those with punishment capabilities. The key contribution is characterizing games with unique CCE or IRCP solutions and establishing structural connections between these concepts, while providing new uniqueness conditions for classical equilibria that don't require dominant strategies, with applications to collusion-proof mechanism design.
