Mapping the causal structure of price formation in Texas's transitioning electricity market
arxiv • Shiva Madadkhani, Nils Sturma, Mathias Drton, Svetlana Ikonnikova • 2026-04-15
Score: 20 electricity market, wholesale electricity, renewable integration
This paper investigates how the causal drivers of wholesale electricity price formation are evolving in Texas's rapidly transforming electricity market, particularly with large-scale renewable integration. Using causal discovery methods applied to supply- and demand-side price determinants, the authors find that wind generation has displaced natural gas as the dominant causal driver of day-ahead prices, with effects over 3 times larger than gas prices, while also redistributing congestion costs across regions in seasonal patterns. The key contribution is demonstrating that traditional gas-price-driven market models are obsolete in renewable-heavy systems, requiring time-varying causal frameworks to capture the complex, region-specific dynamics of price formation during the energy transition.
Truthful Production Uncertainty in Electricity Markets: A Two-Stage Mechanism
arxiv • Shobhit Singhal, Lesia Mitridati, Licio Romao • 2026-04-02
Score: 13 market clearing, electricity market
This paper addresses how electricity markets can better incorporate production uncertainty from renewable sources by designing a mechanism where generators truthfully report their uncertainty distributions, not just point forecasts. The methodology develops a theoretical two-stage mechanism extending Vickrey-Clarke-Groves (VCG) payments where producers report forecast distributions day-ahead and realized production real-time, proving incentive compatibility and individual rationality properties. The key contribution shows this mechanism reduces total system costs by enabling market operators to optimally account for balancing costs in day-ahead dispatch and ancillary service procurement, validated through an electricity market case study.
Carbon Regulation and Competition in the European Airline Industry
arxiv • Ertian Chen, Lichao Chen, Lars Nesheim • 2026-03-29
Score: 13 carbon pricing, emissions trading
This paper examines how EU carbon pricing affects competition and welfare in European aviation markets using a structural two-stage game model where airlines endogenously choose route entry, frequencies, and prices estimated on European market data. The methodology combines demand estimation with supply-side modeling to simulate counterfactual carbon tax scenarios and a hypothetical low-cost carrier merger. Key findings show highly asymmetric impacts: consumer surplus falls up to 25% overall (90% in medium-haul markets), airline profits decline 8-45%, but short-haul markets experience net welfare gains as capacity reallocates toward shorter routes, with carbon revenues ($0.9-3.1B) and avoided emissions benefits ($0.5-1.4B) partially offsetting losses.
Lexicographic Robustness and the Efficiency of Optimal Mechanisms
arxiv • Ashwin Kambhampati • 2026-04-07
Score: 11 mechanism design, screening
This paper addresses how to design robust mechanisms when the economic environment is uncertain by proposing a lexicographic refinement of the standard maxmin robustness criterion. The methodology is theoretical, developing the concept of "proper robustness" as the strongest lexicographic refinement and characterizing optimal mechanisms in screening, auction, and public goods environments. The key finding is that proper robustness selects ex post efficient mechanisms in screening and auction settings, but in public goods provision it identifies specific optimal inefficiencies that worsen significantly in large economies.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite acknowledging catastrophic AGI risks, contradicting standard real options theory that predicts investment delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs are globally shared, the risk terms cancel out of equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where rational actors deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that competitive dynamics can override safety considerations in winner-take-all races with systemic risks, and that only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore rational delay incentives.
