Information Design and Mechanism Design: An Integrated Framework
arxiv • Dirk Bergemann, Tibor Heumann, Stephen Morris • 2026-01-24
Score: 18 mechanism design, auction theory, screening
This paper examines how to optimally design both mechanisms (allocation rules) and information structures simultaneously in screening environments with quasilinear utility. Using majorization theory and quantile functions, the authors show that both problems reduce to maximizing linear functionals subject to majorization constraints, where mechanism design involves choosing allocations majorized by inventory and information design involves choosing structures majorized by priors. The key finding is that when jointly optimizing mechanisms and information structures, pooling of values and allocations is always optimal, providing a unified framework that extends classical auction theory to information design settings.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper examines why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite catastrophic risks from AGI misalignment, which contradicts standard real options theory that predicts investment delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs are shared globally, the risk term cancels out of equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where competitive pressures force early AGI deployment despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore option value and halt the race, as warning shots fail to deter acceleration due to winner-takes-all dynamics.
Dynamic Mechanism Design without Monetary Transfers: A Queueing Theory Approach
arxiv • Zihao Li, Xuandong Chen • 2026-01-28
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines how to optimally allocate randomly arriving agents and goods when monetary transfers are unavailable but costly type verification is possible. Using a queueing-theoretic framework, the authors characterize the steady-state optimal mechanism as a dynamic threshold policy where admission and allocation decisions depend on state-dependent type cutoffs and current queue lengths. The key contribution is showing that optimal allocation without transfers requires dynamic, history-dependent thresholds rather than static rules, with applications to public housing, grants, and internal capital allocation.
The Algorithmic Barrier: Quantifying Artificial Frictional Unemployment in Automated Recruitment Systems
arxiv • Ibrahim Denis Fofanah • 2026-01-20
Score: 9 screening, information asymmetry
This paper investigates whether automated recruitment systems using keyword-based screening artificially increase frictional unemployment by creating inefficient job-candidate matching. The authors use controlled simulations to compare traditional keyword screening with semantic matching based on high-dimensional vector representations of resumes and job descriptions. The key finding is that semantic matching substantially improves recall and matching efficiency without sacrificing precision, suggesting that outdated algorithmic hiring practices contribute significantly to labor market frictions and that better semantic alignment could reduce unemployment duration.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses the research question of how to select optimal intended uses for underperforming real estate assets requiring redevelopment, treating this as a strategic option selection problem rather than purely design or financial optimization. The methodology combines real options theory with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to create a decision framework that evaluates redevelopment alternatives across multiple dimensions including expected economic value, risk, complexity, and time-to-income while preserving managerial flexibility. The key contribution is an integrated analytical framework that operationalizes option value preservation through disciplined ex-ante screening, demonstrating through illustrative cases that this approach reduces over-complexification and use-demand misalignment in redevelopment decisions.
