Salience and (Non-)Buyer's Remorse: Optimal Nonlinear Pricing with Cognitively Constrained Consumers -- by Aaron L. Bodoh-Creed, Brent R. Hickman, John A. List, Ian Muir, Gregory K. Sun
nber
Score: 9 self-selection, screening
This paper examines how behavioral biases affect firms' ability to extract surplus through nonlinear pricing when consumers fail to optimally self-select into subscription contracts. Using large-scale field experiments with Lyft's subscription service, the authors develop a structural model incorporating salience failures, forecast errors, and impulsivity, and employ a novel identification strategy treating subscription uptake as one-sided noncompliance in a binary-instrument framework with LATE methods. The key finding is that while optimal nonlinear pricing can generate substantial gains over linear pricing, these gains are highly sensitive to systematic deviations from rational choice—particularly salience failures and demand forecast errors—requiring firms to coordinate optimal contract design with strategies that address consumer cognitive constraints.
