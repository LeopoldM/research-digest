Principal-agent problems with adverse selection: A stochastic target problem formulation
arxiv • Guillermo Alonso Alvarez, Ibrahim Ekren, Liwei Huang • 2026-05-01
Score: 16 self-selection, screening, adverse selection
This paper examines a principal-agent problem with adverse selection where the principal must offer a single contract without allowing self-selection among multiple contract options. The methodology combines stochastic optimal control theory with target problem formulation, reformulating the agent's optimization as a stochastic target problem and the principal's problem as optimal control under partial information with state constraints. The key contribution is characterizing the "credible domain" of feasible outcomes, which enables solving the constrained optimization problem and provides a method to evaluate the value of traditional screening contracts relative to the single-contract approach.
A Truthful Multiunit Profit-Optimal Mechanism for Synthesizing Social Laws
arxiv • Jun Wu, Jian Huang, Chongjun Wang • 2026-05-16
Score: 16 mechanism design, procurement auction, optimal mechanism
This paper addresses Social Law Synthesis (SLS) in multi-agent systems by formulating it as a Bayesian single-parameter procurement auction where agents strategically bid to participate in synthesized social laws expressed in Alternating-time Temporal Logic (ATL). The methodology combines mechanism design theory with computational approaches, proving a representation lemma for valuations under alternating bisimulation, reducing payment computation to allocation problems, and encoding ATL semantics as integer linear programming constraints despite the FP^NP-completeness of allocation determination. The key contribution is the PO-ASL mechanism that achieves truthfulness, individual rationality, and profit-optimality for multi-unit social law synthesis, providing both theoretical guarantees and computational tractability through standard ILP solvers.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This study addresses how to select optimal future uses for underperforming real estate assets that require redevelopment due to structural demand-supply misalignment rather than cyclical factors. The methodology combines real options theory with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to create a framework that evaluates redevelopment alternatives across dimensions of economic value, risk, complexity, and time-to-income while preserving managerial flexibility. The key contribution is operationalizing option value preservation through systematic ex-ante screening that treats redevelopment as strategic option selection rather than purely financial optimization, thereby reducing project complexity and use-demand misalignment.
Strategic Inertia and Institutional Change:A Behavioral Model of Price Reforms versus Action Deletion
arxiv • Madjid Eshaghi Gordji, Mohammadali Berahman, Hasti Eshaghi • 2026-05-20
Score: 7 climate policy
This paper examines why inefficient institutions persist despite available superior alternatives by developing a quantal response equilibrium model with status-quo bias where players face fixed switching costs from inherited default actions. Using theoretical analysis in a binary coordination game framework, the authors compare price-based reforms (taxing the default action) versus regulatory bans (deleting the default action entirely). The key finding is that while price reforms require a threshold tax level to induce transition (regardless of bounded rationality), banning the inefficient action always forces adoption of the superior equilibrium and yields higher expected welfare than any finite tax when the superior equilibrium is Pareto-dominant.
Breaking Status-Quo Inertia in Living Temporal Games: Dynamic Intervention, Implementation, and Structural Design
arxiv • Madjid Eshaghi Gordji, Ali Jabbari, Mohammad Ali Berahman, Esmaiel Abounoori • 2026-05-18
Score: 7 mechanism design
This paper examines how a planner can design dynamic interventions to overcome inefficient status-quo equilibria in continuous-time stochastic games where agents control their activity states on temporal networks. The methodology combines game-theoretic analysis with mechanism design, proving threshold theorems for intervention effectiveness and constructing specific mechanisms. Key contributions include: (1) a structural dominance result showing that network modifications (replacing continuous-flow with discrete-transport edges) can eliminate inefficient equilibria when price-based transfers with any finite budget cannot, and (2) an impossibility theorem for direct mechanisms under private information, along with a second-best dynamic pivot mechanism that achieves bounded deficit.
