Information Design and Mechanism Design: An Integrated Framework
arxiv • Dirk Bergemann, Tibor Heumann, Stephen Morris • 2026-01-24
Score: 18 mechanism design, auction theory, screening
This paper examines how to jointly optimize mechanism design (allocation rules) and information design (information structures) in screening environments with quasilinear utility. Using majorization theory and quantile functions, the authors show both problems reduce to maximizing linear functionals subject to majorization constraints, with the joint optimization becoming a bilinear program. The key finding is that pooling of values and allocations is always optimal when simultaneously designing mechanisms and information structures, unifying and extending classical auction theory results while providing new welfare insights.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper investigates why the US and China are accelerating AI investment despite catastrophic AGI risks, contrary to standard real options theory which predicts investment delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when failure costs include a globally-shared systemic ruin parameter (rather than just sunk investment costs), the risk terms cancel out of players' equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where rational actors deploy AGI early despite negative risk-adjusted NPV. The key contribution is demonstrating that competitive races involving existential risks require internalizing ruin costs through liability mechanisms to restore proper investment timing incentives.
Dynamic Mechanism Design without Monetary Transfers: A Queueing Theory Approach
arxiv • Zihao Li, Xuandong Chen • 2026-01-28
Score: 9 mechanism design, optimal mechanism
This paper examines how to optimally allocate resources when agents and goods arrive stochastically and monetary transfers are prohibited, with agents having private information about their value to the principal. Using a queueing theory framework where either agents or goods can be queued at a flow cost and agent types can be verified at a cost, the authors characterize the steady-state optimal mechanism. The key contribution is showing that the optimal mechanism is a dynamic threshold policy where admission and allocation thresholds depend on the current queue state, with applications to public programs like housing allocation and organizational capital budgeting where monetary transfers are infeasible.
The Algorithmic Barrier: Quantifying Artificial Frictional Unemployment in Automated Recruitment Systems
arxiv • Ibrahim Denis Fofanah • 2026-01-20
Score: 9 screening, information asymmetry
This paper investigates how automated recruitment systems using deterministic keyword-based screening contribute to artificial frictional unemployment by creating semantic mismatches between job seekers and vacancies. Using controlled simulations, the authors compare traditional keyword screening with semantic matching based on high-dimensional vector representations of resumes and job descriptions. The key finding is that semantic matching substantially improves recall and matching efficiency without sacrificing precision, suggesting significant welfare gains from upgrading outdated hiring algorithms that currently impose artificial barriers in labor market matching.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses the research question of how to systematically select optimal uses for underperforming real estate assets during redevelopment processes. The methodology combines real options theory with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to create a decision framework that evaluates potential uses across dimensions of economic value, risk, complexity, and time-to-income while preserving managerial flexibility. The key contribution is demonstrating that treating redevelopment as strategic option selection rather than pure financial optimization reduces misalignment between intended use and market demand while maintaining option value through disciplined ex-ante screening.
