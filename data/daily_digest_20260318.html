Prices vs. Quantities: Robust Regulation
arxiv • Zi Yang Kang • 2026-03-16
Score: 11 mechanism design, screening
This paper examines the optimal choice between price and quantity instruments for regulating consumption externalities when a regulator faces uncertainty about the correlation between individual demand and marginal externalities, using a robust mechanism design approach that maximizes worst-case welfare. The methodology is theoretical, employing mechanism design theory under uncertainty with worst-case welfare evaluation. The key finding is that quantity controls (floors for positive externalities, ceilings for negative externalities) are optimal under correlation uncertainty, while uniform taxes/subsidies can be optimal when the correlation sign is known, providing a welfare-theoretic rationale for the observed prevalence of non-price regulatory policies.
The Suicide Region: Option Games and the Race to Artificial General Intelligence
arxiv • David Tan • 2025-12-08
Score: 11 mechanism design, real options
This paper investigates why the US and China are accelerating AGI development despite catastrophic risks, contrary to standard real options theory that predicts delay under extreme uncertainty. Using a continuous-time preemption game with endogenous existential risk, the authors show that when catastrophic failure costs are shared globally and correlated with development speed, the risk terms cancel out of players' equilibrium conditions, creating a "suicide region" where rational actors deploy dangerous AGI systems despite negative risk-adjusted returns. The key contribution is demonstrating that competitive pressures can override safety considerations in winner-takes-all races with existential stakes, and that only internalizing ruin costs through private liability can restore rational delay incentives.
Existence of Equilibrium Mechanisms in Generalized Principal-Agent Problems with Interacting Teams
arxiv • Brian Roberson • 2026-02-23
Score: 9 mechanism design, principal-agent
This paper examines the existence of equilibrium in multi-principal mechanism design games where each principal designs incentives for their own team while facing strategic spillovers from other teams' mechanisms. The authors develop a novel theoretical approach that simultaneously tracks truthful outcome distributions and feasible deviation sets to address discontinuities in incentive-compatible mechanism correspondences that can prevent equilibrium existence (as in Myerson 1982). The key contribution is establishing general sufficient conditions for equilibrium existence in these complex multi-principal environments, providing theoretical foundations for analyzing team production and agency problems with strategic interactions across organizations.
A Real-Options-Aware Multi-Criteria Framework for Ex-Ante Real Estate Redevelopment Use Selection
arxiv • Roberto Garrone • 2026-01-15
Score: 9 screening, real options
This paper addresses how to select optimal intended uses for underperforming real estate assets requiring redevelopment, treating this as a strategic option selection problem rather than traditional design optimization. The methodology combines real options theory (to account for irreversibility and managerial flexibility) with multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) to evaluate alternatives across dimensions of economic value, risk, complexity, and time-to-income. The key contribution is a decision-analytic framework that preserves option value through systematic ex-ante screening, demonstrated to reduce over-complexification and use-demand misalignment across different asset types and urban contexts.
Self-Confirming Mechanisms
arxiv • Zhiming Feng, Qingmin Liu • 2026-03-13
Score: 7 mechanism design
This paper examines mechanism design when the designer must learn agents' type distributions from the mechanism's own outcomes rather than knowing them a priori. The authors develop a theoretical framework using self-confirming equilibrium concepts and establish a "fictitious revelation principle" showing any incentive-compatible mechanism can be represented as a direct mechanism with filtered type reports. In the monopoly application, they prove that dominant-strategy self-confirming mechanisms are equivalent to posted-price mechanisms with locally revenue-maximizing prices, providing a foundation for why simple pricing is often optimal under learning constraints.
